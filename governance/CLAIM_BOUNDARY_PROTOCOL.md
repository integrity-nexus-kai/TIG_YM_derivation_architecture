# Purpose

This document defines the canonical rules for limiting, classifying, and controlling claims within the TIG theorem-oriented research architecture.

Its purpose is to prevent premature theorem escalation, overinterpretation, and uncontrolled extension beyond established dependencies.

---

# Core Principle

No TIG claim may exceed the rigor of its supporting dependency chain.

A statement is admissible only if its required definitions, assumptions, and prior structures are explicitly available.

---

# Claim Classes

All claims must belong to exactly one of the following classes:

1. Definition
2. Structural Hypothesis
3. Theorem Candidate
4. Partial Result
5. Rigorous Result
6. External Speculation

---

# Class I — Definition

A definition introduces terminology.

It must not imply physical truth, theorematic validity, or empirical confirmation.

---

# Class II — Structural Hypothesis

A structural hypothesis proposes a possible relation between admissible structures.

It must remain explicitly conditional.

---

# Class III — Theorem Candidate

A theorem candidate identifies a statement that may become provable once all dependencies are closed.

It is not a theorem.

---

# Class IV — Partial Result

A partial result is a statement with limited derivation under explicitly stated assumptions.

Its domain of validity must be stated.

---

# Class V — Rigorous Result

A rigorous result requires:

- precise definitions,
- explicit assumptions,
- valid derivation,
- no circular dependencies,
- and clear scope.

---

# Class VI — External Speculation

External speculation includes connections to:

- Yang–Mills theory,
- confinement,
- quantum gravity,
- physical mass generation,
- cosmology,
- or experimental prediction.

Such claims are not part of the canonical TIG core unless explicitly promoted through dependency closure.

---

# Forbidden Claim Escalation

The following transitions are forbidden without proof:

- structural hypothesis → theorem
- effective restriction → spectral gap
- admissibility → physical law
- operator terminology → rigorous operator theorem
- TIG structure → Yang–Mills solution

---

# Required Claim Format

Every non-definitional claim must be written in one of the following forms:

- “This document defines the hypothesis that...”
- “This structure may indicate...”
- “This remains conjectural...”
- “This requires further derivation...”

Unqualified theorematic language is forbidden unless rigorously established.

---

# Limitation Placement Rule

Limitations should be centralized when possible.

Repeated defensive statements may be reduced in later publication drafts, but during research development all documents must clearly mark unsupported extensions.

---

# Canonical Boundary

The current TIG framework may claim:

- structural admissibility architecture,
- critical branch analysis,
- effective asymptotic restriction hypotheses,
- operator-oriented structural candidates.

The current TIG framework may not claim:

- complete quantum gravity,
- rigorous Yang–Mills proof,
- confinement theorem,
- physical mass generation,
- or complete spectral theory.

---

# Recovery Procedure

If a claim exceeds its support:

1. downgrade the claim class,
2. identify missing dependencies,
3. move speculative content to an external or future-work section,
4. restore minimal structural wording.
