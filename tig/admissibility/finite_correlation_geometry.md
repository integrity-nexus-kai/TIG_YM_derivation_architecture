# Purpose

This document defines the hypothesis that admissible TIG geometries possess finite structural correlation behavior near critical regimes.

The objective is to exclude geometrically uncontrolled long-range divergence structures.

---

# Structural Motivation

Critical geometric systems may develop enhanced nonlinear sensitivity.

Without admissibility constraints, this may generate:

- uncontrolled correlation growth,
- infinite structural propagation,
- or asymptotic instability.

TIG proposes that admissibility restricts such behavior.

---

# Finite Correlation Hypothesis

An admissible TIG configuration must preserve finite structural correlation behavior.

This means:

- geometric influence remains structurally bounded,
- admissible branch deformation does not propagate infinitely,
- and critical sensitivity remains controllable.

---

# Effective Interpretation

Finite correlation is interpreted structurally.

The framework does NOT assume:

- microscopic lattice discretization,
- quantized spacetime cells,
- or explicit quantum field operators.

Instead, admissibility itself constrains correlation propagation.

---

# Correlation Compression Near Criticality

Near the critical discriminant regime:

\[
\Delta \rightarrow 0,
\]

branch compression increases.

This may enhance nonlinear coupling while simultaneously reducing admissible propagation sectors.

Thus:

criticality may geometrically compress admissible correlation behavior.

---

# Structural Admissibility Principle

TIG assumes:

Only structurally admissible sectors preserve finite correlation compatibility.

Non-admissible sectors are excluded from the effective geometric description.

---

# Stability Interpretation

Finite correlation behavior may act as a structural stabilizer.

Admissible geometry therefore avoids unrestricted correlation amplification.

This remains conjectural at the current stage.
