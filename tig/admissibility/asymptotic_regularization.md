# Purpose

This document formalizes the hypothesis that admissible TIG structures exhibit asymptotically regulated geometric behavior near structural criticality.

The objective is to characterize admissible non-divergent structural sectors.

---

# Structural Principle

TIG assumes that not every formally algebraic configuration is geometrically admissible.

Admissibility imposes structural constraints on asymptotic behavior.

---

# Critical Structural Regime

Near the discriminant-degenerate regime

\[
\Delta \rightarrow 0,
\]

ordinary perturbative separation weakens.

This may induce:

- enhanced structural sensitivity,
- nonlinear branch coupling,
- and asymptotic instability risks.

---

# Admissibility Hypothesis

An admissible TIG configuration must avoid uncontrolled asymptotic divergence.

This means:

- admissible branches remain structurally bounded,
- effective geometric behavior remains finite,
- and branch deformation does not generate unrestricted structural amplification.

---

# Structural Regularization

The TIG framework proposes that admissibility itself acts as a structural regularization mechanism.

Thus:

- inadmissible divergent sectors are excluded,
- while admissible sectors remain geometrically controllable.

---

# Effective Geometric Interpretation

The asymptotic regularization principle does NOT assume:

- explicit ultraviolet quantization,
- discrete spacetime,
- or microscopic lattice structure.

Instead, regularization is interpreted structurally through admissibility constraints.

---

# Branch Compression

Near criticality:

- branch separation decreases,
- nonlinear sensitivity increases,
- and admissible geometry becomes compressed.

Admissibility may therefore restrict the allowed asymptotic sector dynamically.

---

# Structural Stability Hypothesis

A central TIG hypothesis is:

Only structurally admissible branches remain asymptotically stable under critical deformation.

This remains conjectural at the current stage.
