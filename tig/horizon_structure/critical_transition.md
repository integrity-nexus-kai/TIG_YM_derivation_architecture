# Purpose

This document formalizes the critical structural transition appearing in the TIG framework.

The purpose is to characterize the branch-merging regime of the TIG horizon equation.

---

# Structural Equation

The central TIG equation is

\[
x^3 - x^2 + \beta^3 = 0.
\]

---

# Critical Condition

Criticality occurs when:

\[
P(x,\beta)=0
\]

and simultaneously

\[
\frac{\partial P}{\partial x}=0.
\]

---

# Derivative Structure

For

\[
P(x,\beta)=x^3-x^2+\beta^3,
\]

the derivative is

\[
\frac{\partial P}{\partial x}=3x^2-2x.
\]

Thus criticality requires

\[
x(3x-2)=0.
\]

---

# Admissible Critical Point

The admissible nontrivial critical point is

\[
x_c=\frac{2}{3}.
\]

Substitution into the structural equation yields

\[
\beta_c^3=\frac{4}{27}.
\]

Therefore

\[
\beta_c=\left(\frac{4}{27}\right)^{1/3}.
\]

---

# Structural Meaning

At the critical point:

- multiple admissible branches merge,
- local branch separation vanishes,
- and ordinary perturbative descriptions become insufficient.

The critical point therefore defines a structural transition regime.

---

# Degeneracy Structure

Near the critical point:

- admissible branch identity becomes unstable,
- root sensitivity increases,
- and structural deformation becomes nonlinearly amplified.

This indicates the presence of a degenerate geometric regime.

---

# Perturbative Breakdown

Ordinary perturbative expansion around isolated branches may fail near

\[
\beta_c.
\]

This occurs because branch separation collapses at criticality.

---

# Structural Interpretation

The critical transition is interpreted as a geometric admissibility transition rather than merely an algebraic coincidence.

The critical regime separates structurally distinct horizon sectors.
